Our History

1898

On June 13, 1898, Prince Edward School's humble origins unfolded through the efforts of visionary and daring citizens, unaware at the time that this tiny "acorn" would sprout into a majestic "oak." A committee, seeking to establish a suitable school building to accommodate 75 pupils, a Master, and necessary facilities, bestowed the name 'Salisbury Public Undenominational School' upon its precursor. They invited local architects to submit plans, with Mr A C A Cator eventually commissioned to design a structure not to exceed £1,000 in cost (though it amounted to £2,000 in the end). The building comprised a single classroom, two small rooms, and a verandah for the Headmaster. Prior to this, the British South Africa Company granted the committee four acres of land on Moffat Street. The Superintendent of Education in the Cape Colony appointed the Headmaster, Mr John Kerr, and the first teacher, Mrs Garcia from South Africa, with salaries of £180 and £90 per annum respectively. Remarkably, Mrs Garcia arrived by coach from Bulawayo since the railway had not yet reached Salisbury. On November 14, 1898, in the middle of the term, Salisbury Public Undenominational School welcomed its first intake, consisting of two boys and two girls, including notable names such as Vivian and Frank Pascoe.

1989-1923

1899 witnessed the enrollment of twenty-one pupils, each paying 10 shillings a month. In 1900, the number of enrolled pupils increased to 27. Unfortunately, frequent disputes between Mr. Kerr and Mrs. Garcia, along with financial issues between the Government and Municipality, clouded the school's future. By 1901, the Government assumed control of the school, renaming it 'Salisbury Public School.' In 1902, under the guidance of the newly appointed Headmaster Mr. H. Duthie, the school welcomed 39 enrolled pupils. In 1903, Mr. D. Blue took on the role of Headmaster. In 1906, the school underwent another name change, becoming 'Salisbury High School.' By 1908, with an enrollment of 52 pupils, the girls relocated, leading to the adoption of the name 'Salisbury Boys' High School.' In 1909, the Municipality presented the first Dux Prizes. In 1910, Mr. L. J. Grant became the Headmaster, and six new classrooms were constructed to accommodate 300 boys. The school welcomed its first boarders, housed at "Brownings" on North Avenue. During this time, the school introduced its first colors and adopted the motto "Tantum Facienda Parum Factum." In 1911, the school launched its first School Magazine, publishing it twice per annum. From 1914 to 1918, during the Great War, Prince Edward School pupils served with distinction. Fifteen pupils, as well as the former Headmaster from 1903 to 1910, Mr. D. Blue, were honored on the Roll of Honour. In 1917, Mr. A. J. Somerville assumed the role of Headmaster. With the return of peace and normality in 1918, the school resumed its regular activities. In 1920, inter-house activities were introduced, and four houses named Jameson, Rhodes, Selous, and Wilson were established, each with its Latin motto. By 1921, the school registered 350 pupils. In 1922, the Old Hararians Association was formed, marking an important milestone. The school's enrollment also reached 384 pupils.

1923-1953

In 1945, the Roll of Honour sadly recorded the sacrifice of 135 pupils who gave their lives for the preservation of world peace and the defeat of international tyranny. During 1940, the school adopted its official song. In 1941, Mr. A. J. Somerville retired as Headmaster after 24 years of dedicated service. He was succeeded by Mr. H. G. Livingston. In 1942, a fund was established to construct an Undenominational Memorial Chapel. In 1947, Mr. H. H. Cole assumed the role of Headmaster, and the school motto was changed to "Tot Facienda Parum Factum." In 1951, the foundation stone for the memorial chapel was laid by Sir Godfrey Huggins. In 1952, Mr. J. A. Slaven was appointed as the new Headmaster. In 1953, the completed chapel was dedicated by the Bishop of Salisbury, the Right Rev Dr. F. R. Paget, along wth the Rev J. Kennedy Grant and the Rev H. Carter.

1953-1980

1956 Mr. J E Gaylard was appointed Headmaster. The first school fete, a gigantic effort, was held to raise £4700 for the building of a new sports pavilion. 1959 New Sports Pavilion was opened on the annual Reunion Day. The new Hobbies Block was built to encourage activities in the Motor, Printing and Photographic clubs. 1962 The John and Robert Downie Museum was built with funds raised from a firework fsir total £2500. 1963 Establishment of main library and open air gymnasium. 1965 Mr. E C.C Owen appointed headmaster. Prince Edward air cadets reformed. 1966 The Observatory was erected for the astronomy club founded in 1957. 1968 Mr. M R S Falconer appointed Headmaster. 1972 Mr. R.A. Suttle appointed Headmaster. 1973 The Harry Robinson Study Centre (presented to the school 75th anniversary gift), was planned. Completion of the administration block and School Mini Arboretum was established. 1974 Closing of the traditional entrance to the school in Prince Edward Street, and a new entrance opened in North Avenue. 1975 Harry Robinson Study Centre was opened. Metal workshop was established. 1976 Two new Squash Courts were built. 1977 Sixth Form Study Centre Library opened. New staff room occupied. Four new tennis courts opened. 1978 80th Anniversary. 1979 3 new tennis courts were opened. 1980 New era dawns with registration of first nonwhite boys to the school. 1980 National Independence Year. 1983 Mr. B Chimbunde was appointed Headmaster School Enrolment 1352 pupils. 1986 Mr. C R Barnes appointed Headmaster

1980-1992

1406 students enrolled. 1987 1423 pupils registered. 1988 90th Anniversary celebrated. Main Sixth Form Library extended to seat 120 pupils His Excellency the President R G Mugabe attended 90th Anniversary Speech Day as Guest of Honour First PLO Computer Camp staged at PE. Number of pupils 1450. Guest of Honour Speech Day His Excellency The Ambassador of the Arab Republic of Egypt Dr. Badawi. PE won the Inter Schools’ Athletics which they hosted at the National Sports Stadium. Second PLO Computer Camp staged. Grant of Japanese Instruments to PE School. Music Department formed under Ms. L Roberts. Since 1986 PE has staged the National Schools’ Rugby, Hockey, Cricket and Basketball Festivals. 1991 – A particularly memorable year with a visit to Prince Edward by her Majesty Queen Elizabeth II, accompanied by Prince Phillip, Duke of Edinburgh, on October 11. Her majesty planted a tree in the school’s arboretum to mark the occasion. Commonwealth Heads of Government Ministers (CHOGM) met in Harare and played a cricket match against the school, their team including celebrities such as Bob Hawke (Australia), John Major Prime Minister of Great Britain), President Gayoon of the Maldives, Clive Lloyd, Graeme Hick, Nawaaz Sharif (Prime Minister of Pakistan), Dr. Kennedy Simmonds (Prime Minister of St Kitts Nevis) and many others. The Speech Day guest of Honour was Sister Katherine Jackson, a highly talented person, renowned for her devotion to blind people, since she herself has been blind since 1985, as well as handicapped people, conservation and environmental issues. Sister Jackson is also well known for her service through Rotary International.

1992-1994

1992 Speech day guests of honour were Mr. and Mrs. Chingoka and the school enjoyed an excellent academic year with a 90% A Level pass rate, a 51 % pass rate from 1924 entries at 0 Level, and an 83 % pass rate at ZJC level. The school’s rowing team entered the South African Championships, the first for many years, and Prince Edward Old Boys staged a reunion at Nyanga for boys who attended the school between 1950 and 1962, resulting in a $100 000 donation to the school. 1993 Cultural activities received increased emphasis, particularly in the area of music, where great progress was made in Ethno music studies, alongside improvements in the school’s commitment to academic achievements. Speech Day guests of Honour were Mr. and Mrs. A Made. Mr. Made is an internationally renowned environmentalist, who contributed much to the school’s installation of the only Earth and Weather Global Village Satellite Tracking Station, which became operative at the school during August 1993. In the same year, Prince Edward’s rowing team became South African champions. 1993 also marked the inauguration of the School Development Association, with Dr. B Campbell as its first chairman. Created in addition to the PTA, the School Development Association came into being with the objectives of assisting with the running of the school as well as working towards the provision of a Cultural and Music Centre to mark Prince Edward’s centenary in 1998.

Memories

Too many hundreds nay, thousands of Old Hararians throughout the world, the memory of their younger days especially school days, which was the bulk of their younger lives lingers ever in their memories, and reminiscences. Prince Edward School induces that line of thought, for school was their life, their being and their all. In fact in the words of the poets and intellectuals, school is a never forgotten factor, something to live for, to strive for and sometimes with their country to die for.
